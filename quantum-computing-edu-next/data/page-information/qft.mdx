### QFT Overview

The Quantum Fourier Transform is a quantum operation that applies the
Discrete Fourier Transform to a set of qubits.
<br />

The Discrete Fourier Transform converts a list of complex numbers into a list of frequency components.
The Quantum Fourier Transform acts on amplitudes of a quantum state, revealing details about
periodic signals that may be hidden in the amplitudes.
<br />

Like all quantum operations, QFT is reversable, as no information is lost during the operation.

Reversed QFT is useful in other algorithms like [Quantum Phase Estimation](/circuits/qpe_2_4) and Shor's Algorithm.


### Mathematical Definition

The $2^{n} \times 2^{n}$ unitary matrix defining the QFT operation applied to $n$ qubits is defined as follows:
$$
\text{QFT}_{2^{n}} = \frac{1}{2^{\frac{n}{2}}} \sum_{x=0}^{2^{n}-1} \sum_{y=0}^{2^{n}-1} e^{\pi i \frac{xy}{2^{n-1}}} \ket{x} \bra{y}
$$

Equivalently, QFT can be described by how it acts upon standard basis states:
$$
\text{QFT}_{2^{n}}\ket{y} = \frac{1}{2^{\frac{n}{2}}} \sum_{x=0}^{2^{n}-1} e^{\pi i \frac{xy}{2^{n-1}}} \ket{x}
$$

Notice that QFT can be defined for any positive integer $N$, where $n = \log_{2}N$, but it is only useful for values of $N = 2^{n}$ where $n \in \mathbb{Z}^{+}$.


### Examples

One qubit QFT ($\text{QFT}_{2}$) is equivalent to the Hadamard gate:
$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\begin{bmatrix}
  1 & 1 \\
  1 & -1
\end{bmatrix}
= \text{H}
$$

Two qubit QFT ($\text{QFT}_{4}$):
$$
\frac{1}{2}
\begin{bmatrix}
  1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
  1 & i & -1 & -i \\
  1 & -1 & 1 & -1 \\
  1 & -i & -1 & i
\end{bmatrix}
$$
